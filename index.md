What My First Year of Computer Engineering Taught Me Beyond the Classroom
When I started my journey in Computer Engineering, I expected difficult mathematics, programming 
assignments, and long hours of studying. What I did not expect was how much the semester would 
teach me outside the syllabus itself. Looking back at my first and second semesters, I realize that some 
of the most valuable lessons came from mistakes, pressure, deadlines, and experiences that cannot 
be found in textbooks.One of the biggest differences between school and university was responsibility. 
In school, teachers constantly reminded us about homework, tests, and preparation. University was 
different. Assignments could pile up quickly, quizzes appeared before I felt fully prepared, and 
managing time became my own responsibility.
Since I commute daily to university instead of living in a hostel, my routine was different from many 
of my classmates. A significant amount of time was spent traveling between home and campus. 
Although commuting allowed me to stay close to family, it also meant losing valuable study time that 
hostel students could sometimes use more efficiently. I quickly learned that every hour matters in 
engineering studies.
Unlike many students who preferred group study sessions, I mostly studied alone. Initially, I thought 
this would make things easier because I could move at my own pace and focus on topics that 
interested me. However, studying alone also meant solving problems independently and spending 
hours debugging code or understanding difficult concepts without immediate help from friends.

Programming Fundamentals was one of the courses that introduced me to a completely new way of 
thinking. Learning Python was my first real experience with programming. At first, writing code felt 
strange because computers require instructions with absolute precision. A missing bracket or a small 
typing mistake could stop an entire program from working.
Gradually, I started understanding that programming is not simply about memorizing syntax. It is a 
way of solving problems logically and systematically. Every assignment improved my thinking process 
and taught me how to divide large problems into smaller manageable pieces.While Python became 
comfortable with practice, Database Systems turned out to be the subject that challenged me the 
most during the semester. Understanding normalization, relationships, SQL queries, and database 
design required a different mindset compared to programming.
My biggest weakness during the semester was MySQL. I often struggled with writing complex queries 
and understanding how multiple tables interact with each other. Sometimes a query would produce 
incorrect results, and finding the mistake felt harder than writing the query itself.The practical 
implementation of SQL became one of the most important learning experiences for me. Reading SQL 
commands in slides and actually implementing them in MySQL or SQL Server are completely different 
experiences. Real understanding only came after repeated practice.One lesson that became very clear 
to me was that databases cannot be mastered by theory alone. SQL is a practical skill. The more 
queries you write, the better you become. Looking back now, I wish I had spent more time practicing 
SQL exercises every week instead of waiting until assessments approached.
One of the people who significantly influenced my academic thinking during this period was Dr. Bilal 
Ahmad. His teaching style was different because he often connected classroom concepts with real￾world applications instead of treating them as isolated academic exercises.
What impressed me most was his emphasis on solving meaningful problems using technology. Rather 
than building machine learning models simply for demonstration purposes, he encouraged students 
to work on problems that could have practical value for society.
Medical datasets were often used as examples because medical data is collected with high precision 
and accuracy. This approach helped me understand that engineering is not only about creating 
technology but also about using technology to solve important human problems.
This perspective became particularly important when I started working on my semester project, a 
Heart Disease Prediction System using Machine Learning.
Building a machine learning model was one of the proudest achievements of my early university 
experience. For the first time, I was working on something that combined programming, data 
processing, and prediction algorithms into a single project.
The project allowed me to see how computer engineering concepts connect with healthcare and realworld decision making. Instead of solving textbook exercises, I was working with data that represented 
actual human health conditions.
Although the project was challenging, it increased my interest in machine learning significantly. It also 
showed me how much more there is to learn in fields such as artificial intelligence, deep learning, and 
predictive analytics.
Computer Engineering is a field where learning never truly stops. Every semester introduces new 
challenges, new technologies, and new opportunities to improve.Looking back at my first year, I do 
not remember only the grades or examinations. I remember the late nights, debugging sessions, 
project deadlines, failed queries, successful predictions, and lessons learned from mistakes.
Those experiences taught me something more important than technical knowledge.
They taught me how engineers grow.
